How I Think

My Problem-Solving
Philosophy

I approach every design challenge the way a game designer approaches level design — with systems thinking, player empathy, and iterative experimentation.

🎯
I Start With the User's World
Before touching Figma, I ask: what does the user feel right now? What do they want to feel? Good UX is emotional architecture. Every interaction should reduce friction and increase delight.
⚙️
I Think in Systems, Not Screens
A screen is just a symptom of a system. I map the full journey — onboarding, core loops, edge cases, failure states — before designing a single component. Developers call this "thinking like an engineer."
🎮
Gaming Made Me a Better Designer
3 years in game design taught me that great UX is invisible — you only notice it when it's missing. Progression systems, reward loops, and feedback mechanics from games are now part of every product I design.
🛠️
I Work at the Design–Dev Interface
I write shader code. I understand component states. I know what "impossible to build" actually means. This means zero-translation-loss between design intent and shipped product.
📊
Business Goals Are Design Constraints
Beautiful design that doesn't move metrics isn't design — it's art. I balance aesthetic ambition with conversion, retention, and engagement goals. Every pixel earns its place.
🔁
Prototypes Are Hypotheses
I prototype to learn, not to prove. Rapid iteration with real users surfaces assumptions you'd never discover in meetings. I kill darlings fast and ship better solutions faster.

01 — Challenge
Standing Out in a Saturated Market
Poker apps are commoditised. Players churn fast. TAG needed to feel premium — like a club membership, not a download. The challenge: build perceived value before the first hand.
02 — Research
Understanding Poker Player Psychology
Interviewed 20+ poker players across skill levels. Key insight: casual players want to feel smart; competitive players want status signals. Both groups respond to scarcity and prestige cues.
03 — Design Decisions
The Gold Identity Was a Strategic Choice
The 3D metallic gold logo wasn't aesthetic whim — it was a deliberate signal of exclusivity. Inspired by luxury memberships (AMEX Black, CRED), the visual language communicates "this isn't free-to-play mediocrity." Every asset, from table felt textures to chip animations, reinforces a single emotional truth: you belong here.
04 — Game Systems
GDD: Class-Based Progression
Designed a full Game Design Document covering player classes, XP curves, tournament brackets, and reward calendars. Borrowed mechanic from RPGs: players level up through consistent play, not just wins.
05 — Impact
Experience Architecture
Delivered complete UX flows for onboarding, lobby, table, tournament, and profile. Designed every microcopy, empty state, error message, and celebration moment as part of a coherent emotional journey.

01 — The Challenge
Designing Without a Blueprint
Most mobile game teams start building before they know what they're building. The GDD exists to solve that — a living document that aligns designers, engineers, artists, and producers before a single line of code is written. The challenge: make it comprehensive without being unreadable.
02 — Research & Benchmarking
Studying What Great GDDs Look Like
Analysed GDDs from Supercell, King, and Gram Games. Key insight: the best GDDs aren't exhaustive encyclopaedias — they're decision trees. Every section answers one question: "What does a new team member need to build this system correctly?" That became the north star for structure.
03 — System Architecture
The 6 Pillars Every GDD Must Cover
I structured the GDD around 6 non-negotiable pillars: Core Loop (what the player does every 3 minutes), Progression System (XP curves, level gating, unlock trees), Class & Character System (archetypes, abilities, counters), Economy Design (soft/hard currency, IAP hooks, daily rewards), Narrative Framework (world, tone, dialogue guidelines), and UX Specifications (screen flows, HUD layout, onboarding sequence). Each pillar cross-references the others — change one, you immediately see the downstream impact.
04 — Class System Design
RPG Archetypes in Casual Games
Borrowed the MMO model of tank/healer/DPS and translated it into a casual context. Players choose a "style" early — aggressive, defensive, or collector — which gates different progression paths. This dramatically increased early retention: players felt ownership from session one, not session ten.
05 — Economy Balancing
Designing the Invisible Engine
Modelled currency sources and sinks across 30-day player lifecycles using spreadsheet simulations. Set scarcity parameters to create desire without frustration — the "almost there" feeling that keeps casual players returning. Defined hard caps on daily earn rates to protect IAP revenue without hurting free-to-play experience.
06 — Playtest & Iteration
Testing Assumptions Before They Become Code
Paper-prototyped the core loop and progression system with 15 testers across two skill levels. Key finding: players understood the class system in under 2 minutes when shown a visual decision tree — but took 8+ minutes with text alone. This single insight restructured the entire onboarding UX: visuals-first, text as footnote. The GDD was updated to mandate this principle across all tutorial design.
07 — Narrative Design
Giving the World a Voice
Wrote the tonal guidelines, world mythology, faction lore, and dialogue voice for both titles. Every character has a defined vocabulary range, sentence length limit, and emotional register. This prevents the common problem of NPCs that sound like system messages.
08 — What I Learned
A GDD Is a Design System, Not a Manual
The biggest lesson: GDDs fail when they try to document everything. They succeed when they define principles, not rules. "The core loop should feel rewarding every 3 minutes" is more useful than "Level 1 gives 100 XP." I now write GDDs that ask why before they answer how.
